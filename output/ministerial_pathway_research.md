# Ministerial Approval Pathway for New Pharmacy Locations in Australia

## Comprehensive Research Report
**Prepared: 15 February 2026**

---

## 1. Executive Summary

### What Is the Ministerial Discretion Pathway?

The Ministerial Discretion pathway is **NOT an alternative to the standard ACPA/Pharmacy Location Rules process**. It is a **fallback mechanism** that can only be used **after** a standard Section 90 application has been **rejected** by the Secretary (following an ACPA recommendation against approval).

Under **Section 90A of the National Health Act 1953**, the Minister for Health and Ageing can personally override the Secretary's rejection decision and substitute their own decision approving the pharmacist — but only if satisfied that:

1. **The community would be left without reasonable access** to pharmaceutical benefits; AND
2. **It is in the public interest** to approve the pharmacist.

### Key Takeaway for MJ & Navin
- You **cannot apply for ministerial approval directly** — you must first go through the standard ACPA process
- The ministerial pathway is only triggered after a **rejection**
- It is best suited for locations that **genuinely serve an underserved community** but fail on technicalities of the Location Rules
- For Norwood, Launceston — since it **qualifies under Item 130** — the standard ACPA pathway is the recommended first step. Ministerial should only be your backup plan.
- For locations that DON'T qualify under any standard rule item, you would need to apply, get rejected, then pursue ministerial — a longer but potentially viable path

## 3. The Process — Step by Step

### Standard ACPA Pathway (Must Come First)

1. **Identify the applicable Rule Item** (e.g., Item 130 for distance-based, Item 136 for large medical centres)
2. **Secure legal right to occupy premises** (lease or agreement to lease)
3. **Lodge application** via the PBS Approved Suppliers Portal
4. **ACPA considers** the application at monthly meetings (see meeting schedule)
5. **ACPA recommends** approval or rejection to the Secretary
6. **Secretary decides** (delegate within Department of Health) — can approve if recommended, or reject even if recommended
7. If **approved** → proceed to open pharmacy
8. If **rejected** → options are: (a) Administrative Review Tribunal, (b) Ministerial Discretion request, or (c) both

### Ministerial Discretion Pathway (After Rejection Only)

#### Step 1: Application is Rejected
- Your Section 90 application must have been rejected by the Secretary
- The rejection must be because the application did not comply with the Pharmacy Location Rules (not for other reasons)

#### Step 2: Make a Written Request to the Minister
- Must be made **within 30 days** of receiving the Secretary's rejection notice
- Alternatively, if you appealed to the Administrative Review Tribunal first:
  - Within 30 days after receiving the ART's decision affirming the rejection
  - Within 30 days if the ART appeal was discontinued/withdrawn/dismissed
- If you went to Federal Court: within 30 days of the court's order
- The request must be in the form determined by the Minister (if any)
- **Email for submissions**: 90Apharmacy@health.gov.au

#### Step 3: Minister Decides Whether to Consider
- The Minister has a period (the Guidelines reference 4 months total for the decision) to decide
- The power must be exercised **personally by the Minister** — it cannot be delegated

#### Step 4: Minister's Assessment
The Minister must be satisfied of TWO things:

**Test 1 — Community Without Reasonable Access:**
- "Community" means a group of people that, **in the opinion of the Minister**, constitutes a community
- "Reasonable access" means access that, **in the opinion of the Minister**, is reasonable
- These are deliberately broad and subjective — the Minister has wide latitude

**Test 2 — Public Interest:**
- No legislative definition or checklist
- Pharmacist must demonstrate that approval would serve the public interest
- Involves geographic, demographic, and health service gap analysis

#### Step 5: Decision
- **Within 4 months** after receiving the request, the Minister must personally decide
- If no decision within 4 months: **deemed refusal** (Minister is taken to have decided NOT to exercise the power)
- Outcomes:
  - Minister exercises power → pharmacist is approved (substitutes Secretary's decision)
  - Minister does not exercise power → Secretary's rejection stands
  - If Minister doesn't decide in time → deemed refusal

#### Step 6: After Ministerial Decision
- The Secretary must notify the pharmacist of the Minister's decision
- If approved: treated as a Section 90 approval for all purposes
- If refused: pharmacist can still appeal the original Secretary's decision to the Administrative Review Tribunal (Section 90A(7))
- **The Minister's refusal can also be challenged in Federal Court** (judicial review)

### Can You Do Both ACPA Appeal AND Ministerial Simultaneously?
- **Yes, with caveats.** The legislation allows you to apply for ART review AND request ministerial discretion
- The 30-day clock for ministerial request runs from whichever happens last (Secretary's decision, ART decision, or Federal Court order)
- A ministerial refusal does NOT prevent ART review of the underlying Secretary's decision
- Strategy: You could lodge ART review to preserve rights while also pursuing ministerial

## 4. Criteria & Evidence Required

### What Makes a Strong Ministerial Application

Based on the Bennett Philp Lawyers analysis and the 2025 Guidelines, a strong application requires:

#### A. Defining the "Community"
- Identify a specific group of people who constitute a "community"
- This could be defined by geography (suburb/locality), demographics, or shared characteristics
- Examples that have succeeded: patients of a specific medical centre, opioid-dependent patients, residents of a new development area
- **Tip**: The narrower and more specific your community definition, the easier it is to show they lack access

#### B. Demonstrating Lack of "Reasonable Access"
- Distance to nearest pharmacy (walking, driving, public transport)
- Physical barriers (highways, rivers, hills, lack of footpaths)
- Vulnerable populations who cannot easily travel (elderly, disabled, parents with young children)
- Public transport limitations
- Operating hours misalignment with community needs
- Specialist services not available at nearby pharmacies
- **What has worked**: Bennett Philp succeeded arguing that opioid-dependent patients being served by a pharmacy where other pharmacies didn't provide that service constituted a community without reasonable access — resulting in the Minister approving **5 separate pharmacies on one day**

#### C. Establishing "Public Interest"
- Population growth data
- Demographic analysis (age distribution, health needs)
- Healthcare infrastructure gaps
- Distance and travel time analysis
- Impact on existing pharmacy services
- Economic impact on the community
- Health outcomes data
- **NOT sufficient on its own**: A petition with thousands of signatures is NOT enough. The Minister requires substantive evidence.

#### D. Supporting Documentation
- **Geographic data**: Maps showing pharmacy locations, distances, barriers
- **Demographic data**: ABS census data, population projections
- **Health data**: Chronic disease prevalence, ageing population statistics
- **Letters of support**: From GPs, medical practitioners, local government, community organisations
- **GP/Medical practitioner statements**: Critical — showing that patients need closer pharmacy access
- **Transport analysis**: Public transport routes, walking distances, accessibility
- **Population projections**: Growth forecasts for the area

#### E. What the Minister Uses Against You
Based on case law and practitioner experience:
- The fact that a large proportion of households have a motor vehicle (Minister has used this to refuse applications)
- Existence of other pharmacies within a "reasonable" driving distance
- Adequate public transport options
- Low population density

## 5. Recent Examples / Case Studies

### Freedom Pharmaceutical Pty Ltd v Minister for Health [2021] FCA 213
- **Situation**: Application to approve a pharmacy within a shopping centre medical centre
- **Community**: Patients of the medical centre
- **Access issue**: Would have to leave the medical centre and travel **60 metres** to the nearest pharmacy
- **Outcome**: Minister APPROVED — satisfied that Section 90A(2) requirements were met

### Bennett Philp Client Case (Unnamed, ~2021-2022)
- **Situation**: Similar to Freedom Pharmaceutical — pharmacy in a shopping centre with a medical centre
- **Access issue**: Community would have to leave the shopping centre and travel **1 kilometre** to the nearest pharmacy
- **Outcome**: Minister initially REFUSED (despite the case being arguably stronger than Freedom Pharmaceutical)
- **Appeal**: Client appealed to Federal Court. On the eve of trial, the Minister (a new Minister had been appointed) agreed to reconsider and ultimately APPROVED
- **Key Lesson**: Ministerial decisions are **inconsistent and discretionary** — the same Minister made opposite decisions on similar cases. Legal preparedness is essential.

### Bennett Philp Opioid Patients Case (~2024)
- **Situation**: Multiple pharmacies serving opioid-dependent patients
- **Community argument**: Opioid-dependent patients who relied on specific pharmacies that provided services other pharmacies did not
- **Outcome**: Minister approved **5 separate pharmacies on one day**
- **Key Lesson**: Creative definition of "community" and "reasonable access" beyond just geographic distance can succeed

### What These Cases Tell Us
1. **Consistency is NOT guaranteed** — the Minister's discretion is personal and can vary between Ministers and even between decisions by the same Minister
2. **Distance isn't everything** — even 60 metres was enough to justify ministerial approval in one case
3. **Medical centre co-location** is a strong argument (patients shouldn't have to leave the medical centre)
4. **Specialised services** (like opioid treatment) can define a community
5. **Legal preparedness** is critical — always prepare as if you may need to go to Federal Court

## 6. Application to Norwood, Launceston

### The Situation
- **Location**: Norwood, Launceston, TAS (near Norwood Medical Centre)
- **Population**: ~81,000 in 5km catchment
- **Nearest pharmacy**: 1.87km away
- **GPs**: 7+ at Norwood Medical Centre
- **Area character**: Growing residential area

### Standard ACPA Pathway (Recommended First)
Norwood appears to qualify under **Item 130** of the Pharmacy Location Rules. This should be the primary application strategy because:
- It's the designed pathway for exactly this scenario
- If successful, it's faster and cheaper than ministerial
- No need to go through rejection first
- ACPA meeting turnaround is typically 1-2 months

### Ministerial Pathway Assessment (Backup)
If the Item 130 application is rejected (e.g., on a technicality or contested by nearby pharmacies), a ministerial case would be STRONG based on:

**Community Definition:**
- Patients of Norwood Medical Centre (7+ GPs)
- Residents of Norwood/surrounding suburbs
- Growing population base

**Reasonable Access Arguments:**
- 1.87km to nearest pharmacy is significant for:
  - Elderly patients
  - Mothers with young children
  - Patients who are unwell (leaving a GP consultation)
  - Patients without vehicles
- No pharmacy currently co-located with the medical centre
- Compare to Freedom Pharmaceutical where 60 metres was sufficient

**Public Interest:**
- 81,000 people in 5km catchment — significant population base
- Growing area — increased future demand
- 7+ GPs generating prescriptions — high prescription volume
- Healthcare continuity — GP consultation → immediate prescription fill
- Reduced travel time for vulnerable populations

**Strength Rating: STRONG** — If this went to ministerial, the case would be compelling, especially given the Freedom Pharmaceutical precedent (where 60m was enough). 1.87km for medical centre patients is a much stronger argument.

### Recommended Strategy for Norwood
1. **Apply under Item 130** first (standard ACPA pathway)
2. **Prepare ministerial-quality evidence** from the start (you'll need it for ACPA anyway)
3. If rejected, **immediately file ministerial request** within 30 days
4. Consider simultaneous ART review to preserve all options
5. Engage pharmacy-specialist lawyer to review application before lodgement

## 7. When to Use Ministerial vs Standard ACPA

### Use Standard ACPA When:
- Your location meets the requirements of an existing Rule Item (130-136)
- The facts are clearly in your favour
- You want the fastest path to approval (ACPA meets monthly)
- You want to avoid the cost of legal proceedings

### Consider Ministerial When:
- Your location doesn't meet ANY Rule Item but serves a genuine community need
- You meet most but not all requirements of a Rule Item (e.g., 1.3km instead of 1.5km)
- Your application was rejected on a technicality despite clear community need
- You have strong evidence of a community being underserved
- You're willing to invest in legal support and a longer timeline

### Timeline Comparison
| Aspect | Standard ACPA | Ministerial |
|--------|---------------|-------------|
| Initial application | 1-2 months to ACPA meeting | Must do ACPA first |
| Decision timeframe | ~2 months total | ACPA + rejection + 30 days to request + up to 4 months for Minister = 7-9 months minimum |
| If appealed to ART | Additional 6-12 months | Can run parallel |
| If appealed to Federal Court | Additional 12-24 months | Can follow ministerial refusal |
| **Total if successful at first stage** | **~3 months** | **~9-12 months** |

### Cost Comparison
| Aspect | Standard ACPA | Ministerial |
|--------|---------------|-------------|
| Application fee | ~$3,000-$5,000 | Same (part of ACPA process) |
| Legal costs (optional but recommended) | $5,000-$15,000 | $15,000-$50,000+ |
| Consultant costs | $5,000-$10,000 | $10,000-$30,000 |
| Total estimated cost | **$13,000-$30,000** | **$25,000-$85,000+** |

### Success Rates
- **No published data** on ministerial approval rates
- Based on case law and practitioner commentary, the success rate appears **moderate**
- Success is heavily dependent on the quality of the application and the specific Minister in office
- Minister's decision is **discretionary and personal** — unpredictable by nature
